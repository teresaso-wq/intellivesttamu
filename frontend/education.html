Getting Started

💰

Budgeting Basics

  • Follow the 50/30/20 rule
  • Track spending for 30 days
  • Automate your savings
  • 50% -> Needs (rent, food, bills)
  • 30% -> Wants (dining, fun)
  • 20% -> Savings + debt payoff
  • Best free tools: Mint, YNAB, EveryDollar
🏦

Savings Accounts

  • High-Yield Savings = 4-5% APY
  • Emergency fund = 3-6 months expenses
  • Pay yourself first on payday
  • Best HYSAs right now: Ally, Marcus, SoFi
  • Start with a $1,000 emergency fund goal
  • Then grow to your full 3-month cushion
💳

Credit Score 101

  • 35% is payment history - never miss
  • Keep credit utilization under 30%
  • Check your report free at annualcreditreport.com
  • 300-579 Poor: Get a secured card
  • 580-669 Fair: Pay full balance monthly
  • 670-739 Good: Apply for rewards cards
  • 740+ Excellent: Best rates on loans
💸

Debt Management

  • Avalanche method: pay highest interest first
  • Snowball method: pay smallest balance first
  • Always pay more than the minimum
  • Avalanche saves the most money in interest
  • Snowball builds momentum and motivation
  • Consolidation can lower your rate — compare carefully
  • Never ignore debt — interest compounds fast
🛡️

Insurance Basics

  • Renters insurance ~$15/month — always worth it
  • Health: know your deductible vs. premium tradeoff
  • Auto: liability is required, full coverage is optional
  • HSA pairs with high-deductible health plans
  • Term life insurance is cheap when you are young
  • Umbrella policy adds extra liability coverage cheaply
  • Bundle home + auto for discounts
💡

Side Income & Freelancing

  • Freelancing income = 1099, set aside 25-30% for taxes
  • Platforms: Upwork, Fiverr, Toptal, LinkedIn
  • Passive income: dividends, content, rentals
  • Track all freelance income and expenses for tax deductions
  • Open a SEP-IRA or Solo 401k to save on taxes
  • Start a side hustle aligned with existing skills
  • Reinvest early profits to grow faster

Investing 101

📈

Stocks

  • Ownership stake in a company
  • Higher risk, higher potential reward
  • Best for long-term (5+ years)
  • Beginner picks: VTI, SPY, QQQ (ETFs)
  • Individual stocks: AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL
  • Never invest money you need soon
🧺

ETFs & Index Funds

  • Basket of many stocks in one trade
  • Lower fees than mutual funds
  • Great for beginners
  • VTI -> Total US market
  • QQQ -> Top 100 tech companies
  • SCHD -> Dividend focused
  • BND -> Bonds (lower risk)
🏛️

Mutual Funds

  • Managed by professionals
  • Minimum investment often $1,000+
  • Good for retirement accounts
  • FXAIX -> Fidelity S&P 500 (0.015% fee)
  • VFIAX -> Vanguard S&P 500 (0.04% fee)
  • SWTSX -> Schwab Total Market (0.03% fee)
🏠

Real Estate

  • Requires 20% down to avoid PMI
  • Credit score 720+ = best rates
  • Budget 2-5% for closing costs
  • 1) Build credit above 720
  • 2) Save 20% down payment
  • 3) Get pre-approved for mortgage
  • 4) Budget for closing + moving costs
  • Best savings vehicle while saving: HYSA
₿

Crypto & Alt Assets

  • Highly volatile — only invest what you can lose
  • Bitcoin & Ethereum are the most established
  • Keep crypto under 5-10% of your portfolio
  • Store on hardware wallets for security
  • Crypto gains are taxable — track every trade
  • DeFi and NFTs carry extra risks
  • Dollar-cost averaging reduces timing risk
📋

Bonds & Fixed Income

  • Bonds = loans you give to governments or companies
  • Lower risk than stocks, lower return too
  • I-Bonds protect against inflation (Treasury Direct)
  • T-Bills: short-term US government bonds, very safe
  • Corporate bonds: higher yield, higher risk than gov bonds
  • Bond prices fall when interest rates rise
  • BND ETF is a simple way to own many bonds at once

Market Concepts From Watchlist

📈

Stocks and ETFs Deep Dive

  • A stock is a small ownership stake in a company
  • ETFs hold many assets and trade like one stock
  • Diversification helps lower single-company risk
  • IPO: first time shares are sold to the public
  • Prices move with supply and demand
  • Returns can come from capital gains and dividends
  • Bull market: broad rising prices; bear market: broad declines
  • Volatility measures how sharply prices swing
🧮

Reading Stock Metrics

  • P/E ratio compares price to earnings per share
  • Volume shows trading activity and liquidity
  • 52-week range gives annual context
  • Example: Price $100 and EPS $5 means P/E = 20
  • Dividend yield = annual dividend divided by stock price
  • Chart basics: Open, High, Low, Close, and Change %
  • Market cap = price x shares outstanding
  • Cap buckets: Large-cap ($10B+), Mid-cap ($2-$10B), Small-cap (under $2B)
🏠

Buying a House Checklist

  • Build strong credit before applying
  • Plan down payment and closing costs
  • Compare lenders and loan types
  • Budget for moving costs and a repair buffer
  • Lenders review debt-to-income closely
  • Compare fixed-rate vs ARM mortgage structures
  • Use high-yield savings while building your down payment fund
📉

Technical Analysis Basics

  • Charts show price history and patterns
  • Support = price floor, Resistance = price ceiling
  • Moving averages smooth out short-term noise
  • RSI above 70 = overbought, below 30 = oversold
  • MACD crossovers signal potential trend changes
  • Volume confirms the strength of a price move
  • Candlestick patterns reveal buyer vs. seller pressure
🌍

Global Markets & Macro

  • Interest rate hikes slow the economy and markets
  • Inflation erodes purchasing power over time
  • GDP growth signals economic health
  • Fed meetings move markets — watch the calendar
  • Strong dollar hurts US companies with overseas revenue
  • Emerging markets offer growth but with higher risk
  • Yield curve inversion has historically preceded recessions
⚖️

Risk Management

  • Never invest money you need within 1-2 years
  • Diversify across sectors, asset classes, and regions
  • Rebalance your portfolio once or twice a year
  • Stop-loss orders automatically limit your downside
  • Position sizing: risk no more than 1-2% per trade
  • Correlation matters — assets that move together don't diversify
  • Time in the market beats timing the market

Taxes, Student Loans & Your First Job

🧾

Taxes 101

  • W-2 = employee, 1099 = freelancer/contractor
  • Standard deduction 2024: $14,600 (single)
  • Contribute to 401k/IRA to lower taxable income
  • File by April 15 every year
  • Free filing: IRS Free File if under $79,000/year
  • Capital gains tax: 0% if income under $47,025
  • Withholdings on your W-2 affect your refund vs. amount owed
  • Traditional 401(k)/IRA contributions may reduce taxable income now
  • Roth contributions do not reduce current taxable income but grow tax-free
  • Short-term vs. long-term capital gains are taxed differently
🎓

Student Loans

  • Federal loans = lower rates, more protections
  • Income-Driven Repayment caps at 5-10% of income
  • PSLF forgives loans after 10 years public service
  • Never miss a payment — ruins credit fast
  • Refinancing loses federal protections — be careful
  • Check your loan servicer at studentaid.gov
  • Keep contact info current with your servicer
  • Auto-debit can reduce interest slightly on some federal loans
  • Document employment carefully if pursuing PSLF
  • Compare refinance offers only if you no longer need federal protections
💼

First Job Finance Checklist

  • Enroll in 401k on day 1 — get the employer match
  • Set up direct deposit split: 80% checking, 20% savings
  • Get renters insurance (~$15/month, always worth it)
  • Understand your health insurance deductible
  • Start emergency fund before lifestyle inflation hits
  • Review your first pay stub — understand every deduction
  • Match = free money; contribute at least enough to capture the full match
  • Emergency fund target: often 3-6 months of essential expenses
  • HSA eligible? It offers triple tax advantages if used wisely
  • Track net pay vs. gross to budget realistically
💰

Salary Negotiation

  • Always negotiate — most offers have room to move
  • Research market rates on Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, LinkedIn
  • Ask for 10-20% above your target to leave room
  • Never give your salary expectations first — let them anchor
  • Negotiate total comp: base, bonus, equity, PTO, remote
  • Get the offer in writing before giving notice anywhere
  • Counter even if it feels uncomfortable — it's expected
🏥

Understanding Your Benefits

  • Health plan: HMO = lower cost, PPO = more flexibility
  • FSA vs HSA: both save taxes on medical spending
  • Life insurance: employer plan usually 1-2x salary
  • HSA rolls over year to year — FSA often does not
  • Disability insurance replaces income if you can't work
  • Vesting schedule: when employer 401k match is truly yours
  • Review benefits during open enrollment every year
🗂️

Building Your Financial Records

  • Keep tax returns for at least 3-7 years
  • Track all pay stubs and W-2s annually
  • Store important docs digitally and in one secure place
  • Use a password manager for financial account logins
  • Set up account alerts for unusual activity
  • Check your credit report free 3x/year at annualcreditreport.com
  • Create a net worth tracker — assets minus liabilities

  • Bull Market -> Prices rising 20%+

  • Portfolio -> Your collection of investments
  • Diversification -> Do not put all eggs in one basket

🎯 Account Types Cheat Sheet

  • 401(k) -> Employer retirement, get the match
  • Roth IRA -> Tax-free growth, best for young people
  • Traditional IRA -> Tax deduction now, taxed later
  • HSA -> Triple tax advantage for medical costs
  • Brokerage -> Regular investing account, no limits
  • HYSA -> High-yield savings, 4-5% APY

📅 Financial Milestones by Age

  • 20s: Build credit, start 401k, build emergency fund
  • 30s: Max retirement accounts, buy a home, grow investments
  • 40s: Diversify, eliminate high-interest debt, plan college costs
  • 50s: Catch-up contributions, review retirement timeline
  • 60s: Social Security strategy, shift to lower-risk assets

💡 Money Rules of Thumb

  • 50/30/20 rule for budgeting (needs/wants/savings)
  • Save 10-15% of income for retirement minimum
  • Rule of 72: divide 72 by return % to see years to double
  • Emergency fund = 3-6 months of expenses
  • Never carry a credit card balance month to month
  • Invest early — time is your biggest asset

🏦 Broker Comparison Cheat Sheet

  • Fidelity — best overall, great research tools
  • Charles Schwab — solid for beginners and ETFs
  • Robinhood — simple app, good for starter investors
  • Vanguard — best for long-term index fund investing
  • TD Ameritrade (Schwab) — great for active traders
  • All offer $0 commission on stocks and ETFs
