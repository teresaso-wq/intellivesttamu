📚 How Stocks Work: A Beginner's Guide

What is a Stock?

A stock (also called a share or equity) represents a small piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you become a partial owner of that company, known as a shareholder.

How Do Stocks Work?

  1. Companies Go Public: When a company wants to raise money, it can "go public" through an Initial Public Offering (IPO), selling shares to investors.
  2. Stock Exchanges: After the IPO, stocks are traded on exchanges like the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) or NASDAQ, where buyers and sellers meet.
  3. Stock Prices: Prices fluctuate based on supply and demand. If more people want to buy than sell, the price goes up. If more want to sell, the price goes down.
  4. Making Money: Investors can profit in two ways:
    • Capital Gains: Buying low and selling high
    • Dividends: Some companies pay shareholders a portion of profits

Why Do Stock Prices Change?

Stock prices change throughout the day based on:

  • Company performance and earnings reports
  • Economic news and trends
  • Investor sentiment and market psychology
  • Industry developments and competition
  • Global events and geopolitical factors

Key Concepts for Beginners

  • Bull Market: A period of rising stock prices and investor optimism
  • Bear Market: A period of falling stock prices and pessimism
  • Market Volatility: How much stock prices fluctuate over time
  • Diversification: Spreading investments across different stocks to reduce risk

💡 Understanding Key Financial Concepts

Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

What it is: The total value of all outstanding shares of a company.

Formula: Current Stock Price × Total Number of Shares

Categories:

  • Large-Cap: $10+ billion (e.g., Apple, Microsoft)
  • Mid-Cap: $2-10 billion
  • Small-Cap: Under $2 billion

Why it matters: Market cap indicates company size and can influence investment risk and growth potential.

Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E Ratio)

What it is: A valuation metric comparing a company's stock price to its earnings per share.

Formula: Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share

Interpretation:

  • High P/E: Investors expect high future growth (can also mean overvalued)
  • Low P/E: May indicate undervaluation or lower growth expectations

Example: If a stock costs $100 and earns $5 per share, the P/E ratio is 20.

Dividends

What it is: A portion of a company's profits paid to shareholders, usually quarterly.

Dividend Yield: Annual dividend payment ÷ Stock price, expressed as a percentage.

Why it matters: Provides regular income and can indicate a company's financial health and stability.

Volume

What it is: The number of shares traded during a specific time period.

Why it matters: High volume suggests strong investor interest and better liquidity (easier to buy/sell).

52-Week High/Low

What it is: The highest and lowest prices a stock has traded at over the past year.

Why it matters: Helps understand a stock's price range and whether it's near historical highs or lows.

Understanding Stock Charts

  • Open: The price when the market opens
  • High: The highest price during the trading day
  • Low: The lowest price during the trading day
  • Close: The final price at market close
  • Change %: Percentage increase or decrease from the previous close

General Investing Principles

  • Start Early: Time in the market often beats timing the market
  • Diversify: Don't put all your eggs in one basket
  • Long-Term Focus: Stocks tend to perform well over long periods despite short-term volatility
  • Research: Understand what you're investing in
  • Risk Management: Only invest what you can afford to lose
  • Avoid Emotional Decisions: Stick to your investment strategy during market fluctuations
